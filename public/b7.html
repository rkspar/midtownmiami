What You Should Know About the Appraisal Process

When your real estate agent submitted the purchase offer on a home, you most likely made your offer depending on several items like financing, home inspection and appraisal. If your loan is locked in and your prospective home passed its inspection with ease, then you have only one challenge left: the appraisal.





Appraisal Basics

Your lender will choose an appraiser to evaluate your home and you will pay the appraiser’s fee, typically cost between $300 to $400. New rules by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau that went into effect in January require your lender to give you a copy of your appraisal as soon as the mortgage company receives it, or at least three days before your closing. You can waive the right, but it makes sense for you to see it so you have time to review it and understand the information your lender is using to determine how much to lend on the home.

An appraisal is based on similar information real estate agents use for comparative market analysis like:

  • Location
  • Age of Property
  • Interior improvements
  • Square Feet
  • Number of bedrooms
  • Number of bathrooms

These facts about the home will be compared to other homes to come up with your home value. You should review the report with your real estate agent to see which homes were chosen for comparison purposes. Make sure the appraisal report includes accurate information that can add value to the home, such as location within a popular school district or near a transportation center.

Appraisal Value to Buyers

It is important to recognize that an appraisal is not meant to derail your real estate deal. In fact, it can function as a consumer protection for a buyer. If your appraisal comes in higher than the price you are paying for the home, then you benefit immediately because you will have more home equity in the property than you thought. For example, if you are paying $350,000 for a home and the appraiser says it is worth $375,000, you instantly have gained $25,000 in equity.

If the appraisal comes in lower than the sales price, you and the seller will need to accept by the contract you and your real estate agent have negotiated. If your contract is depending on an appraisal, one option you have is to withdraw your offer and have your earnest money deposit returned. The appraisal has saved you from paying too much for the home.

If you prefer to buy the home anyway or have waived the appraisal contingency to make your offer more attractive to the sellers, you have a few other options:

  • Challenge the appraisal with documentation from your real estate agent
  • Pay for a second appraisal, which may or may not come in higher
  • Come up with extra cash to make up the difference between the appraised value and your purchase price
  • Renegotiate the contract, if the seller is willing to agree
  • Ask the seller to finance the gap between the appraisal and the sales price
